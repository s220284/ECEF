The LandscapeWhere we are: AGI between 2027 and 2030

"AI" has become the two most promiscuous letters in the English language. Everything is "AI" now, which makes the term almost useless. The north star for the frontier labs is AGI, artificial general intelligence, and there is no agreed-upon definition. The people most likely to build it (Dario Amodei of Anthropic, Demis Hassabis of Google DeepMind) put the timeline at roughly 2027 to 2030. Their working definition: anyone with a smartphone gets access to a data center full of PhD-level expertise in every discipline, on demand, with agency to act. Plan your next few years against that timeline. Artificial super intelligence follows AGI. That is just how it works.

For scale on the pace: Anthropic went from $19 billion to $32 billion in annual recurring revenue in 45 days. No company in history has made money that fast. New frontier models ship roughly every 49 days.

The VocabularyThe three ways AI gets deployed

Most confusion in the market comes from using one word for three different things. Use these definitions with any vendor who walks into your office.

One call

Task

A single model call. One prompt, one job.

Like: "Summarize this email." "Classify this document."
Mappable

Workflow

A process you could map yourself. A to B to C, with decision points. The workhorse of real corporate AI.

Like: a proposal writer, a post-show audit, a ticket-sales analysis.
Autonomous

Agent

A tool that decides its own path. You give it a goal, it determines how to reach it, calling tools in a loop.

Like: "Buy my CES tickets," handled end to end.

We build a lot of workflows for clients and very few agents. Some Fortune 500 companies are running 20,000 to 30,000 workflows and only a handful of true agents. You call a workflow by writing a sentence. Type "I need to write a proposal for ECEF" and the system recognizes the word "proposal" and runs every step. Nobody gets trained to operate it.

The ArchitectureThe anatomy of a corporate AI system

Four layers. Keep them straight and the whole field gets clearer.

1

Models

Raw intelligence. Many flavors, many biases. You pick the right one for the job. They do nothing for your business on their own.

2

The harness

Everything you build around the model that makes it yours: your databases, permissions, user experience, guardrails, cost controls, and policies. This is the magic and the moat.

3

The system

The model plus the harness. This is your AI platform.

4

Agents

What makes the system appear to act on its own. Orchestrators break a problem into parts and spawn sub-agents (some ephemeral, some persistent) to execute.

One framing to hold onto: this is a word calculator tied to a reasoning engine. It is not alive and it is not sentient. It appears to act on its own, and that choice of words matters.

The One MoveThe prompt to teach your whole team

We have narrowed AI training down to a single sentence. Add it to the end of any prompt:

“
Ask me clarifying questions until you are 95% sure you can accomplish the task I have given you successfully.

The model will ask 15 to 20 questions. Once the problem is fully defined, it solves it well. Ask the wrong question and you are guaranteed the wrong answer.

The Operating ModelStop running "AI transformation." Build a flywheel.

"Change management" and "AI transformation" both imply a beginning, a middle, an end, a fixed budget, and a finish line where you are "done." That is the wrong model. What you want is a culture of continuous adaptation, built as an agentic flywheel that never stops.

  1. EvalWrite the answer key first. Describe exactly what a 100 on this homework looks like.
  2. ContextTell the system its identity. "You are a post-show auditor." "You are a trade-show scheduler."
  3. CapabilitiesGive it tools. Web access, a specific database, a credit card with limits.
  4. GuardrailsDefine what it may not do. "If the answer is not in our database, say you do not know. Do not hallucinate." Your ethics manifesto lives here too.
  5. Agents and sub-agentsAdd the execution layer.
  6. RepeatIt never stops, and every step gets its own eval.

An eval can be as simple as a thumbs up or down, or as rich as a spreadsheet: column one is what you asked, column two is what the system answered, column three is a letter grade, column four is the expert answer you wish it had given. That fourth column flows back into your harness, and your system gets better at your business every single day. You are never teaching ChatGPT or Gemini or Claude. You are teaching your system to use those models for your business.

A ReassuranceYour data is safe

The models do not train on the data you put in the context window. It goes into your memory, not their training set. The risk is the same as storing your files on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud, governed by the same kind of contract that keeps your data sovereign. Anyone too afraid to put proprietary data into a properly contracted AI system should not be using cloud storage either. Secure your own memory and database. That part is your job.

A Buyer's ChecklistBeware of agents

Everyone will come into your office this year selling "agents." Before you buy, ask:

  • Which kind is it? A task, a workflow, or a true agent by the definitions above?
  • What evals run on it, and how does it improve over time?
  • Access control and security. An agent acting on your behalf needs a badge and security, exactly like an employee. Sub-agents need audit trails too.
  • Lifecycle management. If you have 20,000 workflows and two-thirds were used once, how long should they live? Not long.
  • Model-garden policy. Models drift. Between Claude Opus 4.6 and 4.7, the right way to instruct flipped from negative to positive. Someone has to own watching the models.
  • Ethics and compliance. Decide how you want these tools used and hold the line.

The New FrontMarketing in an agentic world

Search is becoming answer. Google is building answer engines (AI Overviews, AI Mode), and zero-click answers have been punishing publishers. The discipline is shifting from SEO to AEO, answer engine optimization, and the new fight is for share of prompt rather than share of mind. How you show up inside a context window is not something the hyperscalers have explained yet. I am not convinced they know.

Watch agentic commerce closely. I demonstrated Atlas (OpenAI's ChatGPT-plus-browser) shopping Instacart at Fairway Market on its own. It worked. It also raised the questions that should keep you up at night. The onion it chose was unbranded. Who made that choice? Instacart? Fairway, based on inventory? OpenAI, based on who is paying? There are at least three new value chains hidden in a single grocery order. And OpenAI ends up with the full record of what I wanted, how I wanted it, who I wanted it for, where I shopped, and what I approved. The amount of consumer surveillance this requires has no analog in our world.

The One That Matters MostThe world is shifting to default-to-distrust

We all default to trust. You will sit down to lunch surrounded by a hundred strangers holding sharp knives and never think about it, because trust is a low-energy state and it has kept us evolutionarily stable. Trust is also exactly what a trade show sells. Trust us to gather the right people. Trust this brand to keep its promise.

AI now produces images you cannot believe, video you cannot believe, audio and text you cannot believe. On New Year's Eve, people stood freezing on the East River shore waiting for a fireworks display they had been promised for weeks. The video from that night shows no fireworks. A complete rug pull. The question is how many rug pulls people endure before they shift to a low-energy state of distrust. Your phone already defaults to distrust for you, ringing only for contacts. When consumers default to distrust across the board, the trust you manufacture at a live event becomes the most valuable thing in the economy, and very possibly the only thing. Treat it accordingly.

The Action ListWhat to do Monday morning

  1. Adopt the Task / Workflow / Agent vocabulary across your team so everyone means the same thing.
  2. Pick one repeatable process (proposal writing, post-show reporting, exhibitor follow-up) and build it as a workflow with a real eval.
  3. Teach the clarifying-questions prompt to every person who touches AI.
  4. Put one person in charge of model-garden policy and evals.
  5. Pressure-test every "agent" vendor with the checklist above.
  6. Start an AEO conversation about how your shows, your brands, and your exhibitors show up inside AI answers.
  7. Treat trust as your core product and build for a world that defaults to distrust.
